# Problem Statement

## The Core Problem

Smallholder agricultural producers in Turkmenistan, Uzbekistan, and Kazakhstan — specifically approximately 180,000 registered small-scale farmers producing export-grade commodities including peanuts, sesame, cotton, and oilseeds — are structurally excluded from direct participation in international commodity markets.

## Evidence

These producers depend entirely on a chain of domestic and regional intermediaries who collectively capture an estimated 55–70% of final export margins (FAO, 2023). A farmer selling export-grade peanuts in the Mary Province of Turkmenistan receives approximately **$0.28/kg** at the farm gate. The same commodity trades at **$1.10–$1.40/kg** on international spot markets (Hamburg, Rotterdam, Shanghai). The margin gap is not agricultural — it is **informational, relational, and infrastructural**.

No accessible digital platform currently provides:
- Real-time international commodity pricing data in Turkmen, Russian, or Uzbek
- A verified network of international buyers with transparent trade histories
- Automated export documentation support (certificates of origin, phytosanitary certificates, customs forms)
- AI-powered demand forecasting calibrated to Central Asian seasonal production cycles
- Mobile-first access suited to low-bandwidth rural environments

## Narrow Customer Segment

**Primary Segment:** Small-scale agricultural commodity producers (1–50 hectare operations) in Turkmenistan, Uzbekistan, and Kazakhstan, aged 25–58, with basic smartphone access (Android, 3G/4G intermittent), currently selling exclusively through domestic middlemen, producing a minimum of 2 tonnes of export-grade commodity per harvest season, and operating without in-house legal or export compliance expertise.

**Secondary Segment:** Agricultural cooperatives (10–200 member farms) in the same geography that aggregate produce for export but lack direct buyer networks and rely on intermediary trading houses.

## Why This Matters Now

- Central Asia's agricultural exports grew 18% year-on-year between 2021–2024 (World Bank, 2024)
- Smartphone penetration in the target region reached 67% in 2025 (GSMA, 2025)
- The EU's 2025 trade facilitation framework expanded preferential access for Central Asian agricultural exporters — creating new demand that intermediaries currently capture
- AI translation and document automation costs have collapsed to a point where a mobile-first, multilingual platform is now commercially viable at small-producer scale

## Problem Statement (Single Sentence)

> Central Asian smallholder producers are locked out of the $4.2 billion regional agricultural export market by an information and infrastructure gap that intermediaries exploit to capture up to 70% of farm-gate margins — a problem that AI-enabled trade platforms are now technically and economically capable of closing.
