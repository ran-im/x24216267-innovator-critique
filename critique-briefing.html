PART B Trust Transfer Audit 15 marks

Reading both artefacts as a prospective smallholder producer from Mary Province, Turkmenistan — the exact primary target segment — the following three moments represent specific trust collapse points:

File: index.html — Testimonials section
"I sold my sesame harvest to a buyer in Düsseldorf at 3.8 times my previous price. SilkAI handled the translation, the contract, and the documentation. Payment arrived in 12 days." — Rashid M., Farmer, Fergana Valley, Uzbekistan

Trust collapses here because a Central Asian producer in the exact target segment — who knows Fergana Valley market conditions and local sesame prices intimately — will immediately recognise that a 3.8× price improvement with 12-day German payment is implausibly precise for a pre-launch platform with no live operations, and the moment "Rashid M." proves unverifiable, every other platform claim becomes suspect by association.

File: index.html — Features section (Document Automation)
"Compliant with 34 importing country requirements"

Trust collapses here because the first producer who generates a document for a specific destination country and has it rejected by customs authorities will experience the operational failure of this precise compliance claim as a direct betrayal — destroying confidence in the document automation service, which is the platform's most operationally differentiating and legally consequential feature.

File: pitch.html — Slide 7: Traction
"847 Waitlist signups across 4 countries… 3 Agricultural cooperative partnerships signed… 2 Government Letters of Intent (TKM + UZB)"

Trust collapses here because any investor conducting standard due diligence who asks to speak with one of the three "signed cooperative partners" or to see a copy of either government LOI — and finds neither can be produced — will conclude that the founding team fabricated traction, ending the investment relationship and potentially triggering legal consequences under securities disclosure obligations.

Were the Errors Severe?

The most severe error is the presentation of fabricated traction metrics (pitch.html, Slide 7) to an investor audience. If presented unqualified to a YCombinator partner or BlackRock representative, these could constitute fraudulent misrepresentation under the Companies Act 2014 (Ireland) or equivalent securities law. This error does not fundamentally change the business thesis — the problem is real, the market gap is real — but it makes the founders legally exposed before the platform has traded a single kilogram.

The hallucinated FAO citation (index.html chart note) is moderately severe. It does not alter the price gap narrative (WorldTopExports data confirms ~$1.37/kg international shelled peanut average vs. low Central Asian farm gate prices), but it signals a failure of intellectual rigour that would surface in any academic or investor review.

The smartphone penetration claim ("67%") is not directly stated in index.html or pitch.html (it appears in the business plan .md file), but GSMA Mobile Economy Eurasia 2024 data shows that Turkmenistan and Uzbekistan significantly exceed the regional average for mobile internet non-usage (28% of those covered do not use mobile internet), with 3G still accounting for over 40% of regional connections. The assumption of 67% penetration for Turkmenistan specifically is materially overstated and would lead to a flawed go-to-market assumption in Phase 1. This finding shapes the Part E position below.

What the Prototype Demonstrates

The prototype implements the complete core value proposition of SilkAI in a working three-step interaction, not a static mockup:

  1. Step 1 — List Your Harvest: A producer fills in a form with crop type, quantity, quality grade, harvest date, location, and current local selling price. All fields are interactive; the quality grade selector uses a button group (not a dropdown) to reduce cognitive load for low-literacy users.
  2. Step 2 — AI Buyer Matching: On submission, an animated loading sequence simulates the AI matching process — cycling through six loading messages ("Analysing crop profile… Searching 847 verified buyer profiles… Ranking by reliability score…") with a real-time progress bar. Three buyer cards then appear with staggered animation: GreenHarvest GmbH (Germany, $0.98/kg, 97% match), PearlNut Import Co. (Netherlands, $0.91/kg, 84% match), and AgroTrade Shanghai (China, $0.85/kg, 71% match). Price uplift vs. the producer's entered local price is calculated dynamically in JavaScript.
  3. Step 3 — Document Generation: After selecting a buyer, the producer sees a trade summary with the calculated total contract value. Clicking "Generate Export Contract" reveals an AI-generated draft contract — fully populated with the entered crop details, buyer identity, agreed price, and Incoterms — formatted as a printable document with signature lines. A legal disclaimer notes that the draft requires professional review before signing.

Design Decisions

The prototype uses the same design language as the landing page (dark/gold/cream palette, Cormorant Garamond + DM Sans typography). All interaction state is managed in JavaScript with no external dependencies or APIs. A disclaimer banner throughout labels it clearly as an alpha demonstration, not a live service. The escrow claim from the landing page is reflected in the contract payment terms but not actively implemented, consistent with the Part A finding that PSD2 authorisation is required before this feature can go live.

PART E Business Modelling 15 marks

Position: (b) Ship — with named conditions

The evidence gathered through red-teaming, pre-mortem analysis, and market research reveals a venture with a real and verifiable problem (the agricultural price gap in Central Asia is supported by FAO FAOSTAT and WorldTopExports data), a defensible solution, and a technically buildable product. However, three failure modes identified in pre-mortem analysis make unconditional 90-day shipping irresponsible.

Pre-mortem — three failure modes if shipped unconditionally in 90 days: (1) Regulatory shutdown: the PSD2-unregistered escrow feature triggers a Central Bank of Ireland enforcement action before launch, forcing a public product recall that destroys early trust. (2) Document failure: a producer in Turkmenistan generates a phytosanitary certificate draft, submits it to state authorities, and it is rejected because the template was generated by AI without country-specific verification — the "34 country compliance" claim becomes a liability rather than a feature. (3) Market assumption failure: go-to-market plans built on 67% smartphone penetration in Turkmenistan encounter the reality of sub-40% penetration and 3G-only infrastructure; cooperative onboarding fails due to connectivity assumptions, and no organic growth occurs in the Phase 1 target market.

Red-team: strongest single objections. A skeptical VC: "You have fabricated traction. You have no real users. Your platform does not exist." A sector regulator (Central Bank of Ireland): "Your escrow feature is a payment service under PSD2. You are not authorised to offer it." A target customer: "The document you generated was rejected at customs. My cargo has been impounded. Who is liable?"

Against this evidence, the position is: ship with the following five conditions, each non-negotiable:

Condition 1: GDPR-compliant data infrastructure deployed and Irish Data Protection Commission registration filed before any public email collection or user onboarding begins.
Condition 2: PSD2-licensed escrow partner contracted, disclosed, and integrated before "Payment Protection Escrow" appears in any marketing material or product interface.
Condition 3: Minimum 50 real, independently verifiable active producers on the platform before any traction metric is shown to investors or mentioned in press.
Condition 4: Export document templates tested, submitted, and accepted by customs authorities in at least two target countries (Turkmenistan and Germany/EU) before "document automation" is offered as a live product feature.
Condition 5: Turkmenistan go-to-market plan revised to reflect GSMA-documented mobile infrastructure reality: cooperative-mediated onboarding via SMS, not smartphone app, as primary acquisition channel for Phase 1.

With all five conditions met, ship. The underlying problem is real, the market is large, and the solution is technically achievable. The conditions are not stalling tactics — they are the minimum viable compliance and trust foundation without which the platform will fail at first contact with the real world.

What We Are Deliberately NOT Doing With This Money — and Why

  • No TV or paid digital advertising (Days 1–180). Cooperative channel CAC ($48) is 10× more efficient than digital CAC in this segment. Paid acquisition burns capital and builds zero organic trust infrastructure. We will not spend on advertising until we have 500 real users who can generate credible referrals.
  • No enterprise sales team. Enterprise buyers (national grain boards, commodity hedge funds) are a Year 2+ opportunity. Hiring enterprise sales in Year 1 before the SME product is proven wastes €200K+ on a channel we are not ready for.
  • No geographic expansion beyond Turkmenistan + Uzbekistan in the first 180 days. The GSMA research revealed that our smartphone penetration assumption for Turkmenistan was materially wrong. We need to understand and solve the actual infrastructure reality of Phase 1 markets before expanding. Kazakhstan expansion begins Day 181 at earliest.
  • No office in Dublin beyond a registered address. The team is distributed (Ashgabat, Tashkent, remote EU). A Dublin office would cost €80,000/year for reputational signalling that adds no producer value. Irish incorporation provides EU regulatory credibility without physical office overhead.
